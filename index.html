The Swimmer's Paradox — Why Easy Must Be Genuinely Easy
Your elite swimming background is your biggest asset and your biggest hidden risk. Your cardiovascular system is exceptional — you can sustain high heart rates that would destroy a typical runner. But tendons, ligaments, and fascia adapt 2–3× slower than your aerobic system. This means easy runs will feel suspiciously easy. That's not permission to go faster — it's a warning. The structural stress accumulates quietly, below the threshold of aerobic fatigue, until it becomes a stress reaction or injury. Every easy day must be genuinely easy: 9:20/mi or slower, fully conversational. Your fitness will express itself on race day, not on Tuesday morning runs.
Coaching Principles
The 80/20 Rule
80% of miles are run at easy, aerobic effort. 20% are hard. This ratio is backed by decades of research on elite endurance athletes. Running too much at moderate intensity — the "gray zone" — accumulates fatigue without maximizing either aerobic development or speed adaptation. Hard days are hard, easy days are truly easy.
The 10% Mileage Rule
Weekly mileage never increases more than 10% from the previous week (excluding recovery weeks). Your aerobic system could handle 50 miles/week today — your tendons cannot. The plan starts at 22 miles and peaks at 28, a 40% increase managed over 6 weeks with built-in recovery.
Tempo Runs & Lactate Threshold
Your lactate threshold pace is the single strongest predictor of half marathon performance. Tempo runs at 8:10–8:30/mi push your LT upward. Each session teaches your body to sustain a higher effort before entering oxygen debt. By peak week, you'll hold race pace at a sustainable metabolic cost.
Strides & Neuromuscular Efficiency
20-second controlled accelerations after easy runs teach your legs to turn over fast without accumulating fatigue. Strides recruit fast-twitch fibers, improve running economy, and maintain speed without the recovery cost of a full speed workout. Neuromuscular tuning — keeping the engine responsive while the miles roll in.
Long Runs & Aerobic Adaptation
Long runs at easy effort build mitochondrial density, improve fat oxidation, and increase plasma volume. The slow pace is the point. Some long runs end with goal-pace miles to rehearse the race-day metabolic shift under glycogen depletion — your most specific race preparation.
Taper Science
Weeks 7–8 reduce volume 20–30% while maintaining intensity. Muscles refill glycogen, micro-tears repair, mitochondria densify, neuromuscular patterns consolidate. You'll feel sluggish early in the taper then suddenly sharp — that's taper madness, and it means the plan is working. Do not add extra miles.

Plan Structure
Phase 1: Weeks 1–3 — Base Building + Race
Establish aerobic infrastructure. Respect connective tissue adaptation. Week 3 is the April 26 race — treated as a strong training run with mini-taper. Mileage: 22 → 24 → 18.
Phase 2: Weeks 4–6 — Development + Peak
Post-race recovery followed by the two highest-volume weeks. Week 6 peaks at 28 miles with the most demanding long run. This is where fitness is built. Mileage: 22 → 26 → 28.
Phase 3: Weeks 7–8 — Taper
Volume drops 20–30%. Intensity maintained. Adaptations consolidate into race-ready fitness. By end of week 8, legs should feel bouncy, fast, and fresh. Mileage: 22 → 16.
